5.1 Security Governance

Security governance defines how an organization manages and controls its security policies, procedures, and decision-making processes.

It ensures security is implemented in a structured and accountable manner.

Guidelines

Guidelines are recommended best practices for performing tasks securely.

They are not mandatory, but they help employees follow consistent security practices.

Example:

Recommended password management practices.

Policies

Policies are formal rules and requirements established by management.

They define what must be done to maintain security.

Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)

Defines how employees can use organizational systems and networks.

Examples:

Internet usage rules

Device usage policies

Prohibited activities

Information Security Policies

Define how sensitive information should be protected.

Examples include:

data handling rules

encryption requirements

Business Continuity Policy

Ensures the organization can continue operations during disruptions.

Disaster Recovery Policy

Defines procedures to restore IT systems after disasters.

Incident Response Policy

Provides guidance on how to respond to cybersecurity incidents.

Software Development Lifecycle (SDLC)

Defines secure development practices throughout the application lifecycle.

Stages include:

planning

development

testing

deployment

maintenance

Change Management Policy

Defines procedures for making system changes while minimizing risk.

Standards

Standards are mandatory technical requirements supporting policies.

Examples include:

Password standards
Access control standards
Physical security standards
Encryption standards

Standards ensure systems follow consistent security configurations.

Procedures

Procedures provide step-by-step instructions for performing tasks.

Examples include:

Change management procedures
Employee onboarding procedures
Employee offboarding procedures
Security playbooks

External Considerations

Organizations must consider external factors when implementing security programs.

These include:

Regulatory requirements
Legal obligations
Industry standards
Local or regional laws
National regulations
International regulations

Examples:

GDPR
HIPAA
PCI DSS

Monitoring and Revision

Security governance requires continuous monitoring and updating.

Policies and controls should be reviewed regularly to address new threats.

Governance Structures

Governance can be organized through different structures.

Boards

Executive leadership responsible for strategic decisions.

Committees

Groups responsible for reviewing and managing security initiatives.

Government Entities

Government agencies responsible for cybersecurity regulation and enforcement.

Centralized Governance

Security decisions managed by a single authority.

Decentralized Governance

Security decisions distributed across multiple departments.

Roles and Responsibilities for Systems and Data

Clear responsibilities are essential for security management.

Owners

Individuals responsible for data or systems.

Controllers

Entities responsible for determining how data is processed.

Processors

Organizations or individuals that process data on behalf of controllers.

Custodians / Stewards

Individuals responsible for maintaining and protecting data.

5.2 Risk Management

Risk management involves identifying, assessing, and controlling security risks.

Risk Identification

Process of identifying potential threats and vulnerabilities affecting systems.

Risk Assessment

Evaluating risk likelihood and potential impact.

Types include:

Ad hoc assessments
Recurring assessments
One-time assessments
Continuous assessments

Risk Analysis

Analyzing the severity of identified risks.

Qualitative Risk Analysis

Uses subjective ratings such as:

high risk

medium risk

low risk

Quantitative Risk Analysis

Uses numerical values to measure risk.

Single Loss Expectancy (SLE)

Estimated financial loss from a single incident.

Formula:

SLE = Asset Value × Exposure Factor

Annualized Loss Expectancy (ALE)

Estimated yearly loss from a risk.

Formula:

ALE = SLE × Annualized Rate of Occurrence

Annualized Rate of Occurrence (ARO)

Number of times an incident is expected to occur annually.

Probability

Likelihood of an event occurring.

Likelihood

Chance that a threat will exploit a vulnerability.

Exposure Factor

Percentage of asset loss during an incident.

Impact

Damage caused by a security incident.

Risk Register

Document tracking all identified risks.

It includes:

Key risk indicators
Risk owners
Risk thresholds

Risk Tolerance

Level of risk an organization is willing to accept.

Risk Appetite

Overall approach toward risk.

Types include:

Expansionary
Conservative
Neutral

Risk Management Strategies

Organizations manage risk using several strategies.

Risk Transfer

Transferring risk to another party.

Example:

Cyber insurance.

Risk Acceptance

Accepting risk without mitigation.

Includes:

Exemption
Exception

Risk Avoidance

Eliminating risk by removing the risky activity.

Risk Mitigation

Reducing risk using security controls.

Risk Reporting

Communicating risk information to management.

Business Impact Analysis (BIA)

Determines the potential impact of disruptions on business operations.

Recovery Time Objective (RTO)

Maximum acceptable downtime for systems.

Recovery Point Objective (RPO)

Maximum acceptable data loss measured in time.

Mean Time to Repair (MTTR)

Average time required to fix a system.

Mean Time Between Failures (MTBF)

Average time between system failures.

5.3 Third-Party Risk Management

Organizations must evaluate security risks from vendors and partners.

Vendor Assessment

Security evaluation of third-party vendors.

Methods include:

Penetration testing
Right-to-audit clauses
Internal audit evidence
Independent assessments
Supply chain analysis

Vendor Selection

Process of choosing vendors securely.

Includes:

Due diligence
Conflict of interest review

Agreement Types

Contracts defining responsibilities between organizations and vendors.

Service Level Agreement (SLA)

Defines expected service performance.

Memorandum of Agreement (MOA)

Formal agreement between organizations.

Memorandum of Understanding (MOU)

Non-binding agreement outlining cooperation.

Master Service Agreement (MSA)

Contract defining long-term service terms.

Work Order / Statement of Work (SOW)

Detailed description of project work.

Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA)

Legal agreement protecting confidential information.

Business Partners Agreement (BPA)

Agreement defining responsibilities between partners.

Vendor Monitoring

Continuous monitoring of vendor security performance.

Security Questionnaires

Vendors complete security questionnaires to assess risk.

Rules of Engagement

Define boundaries and procedures during security testing.

5.4 Security Compliance

Compliance ensures organizations follow laws, regulations, and internal policies.

Compliance Reporting

Organizations must report compliance status.

Types include:

Internal reporting
External regulatory reporting

Consequences of Non-Compliance

Failure to comply may result in:

Fines
Sanctions
Reputational damage
Loss of business license
Contractual penalties

Compliance Monitoring

Ensuring continuous compliance with regulations.

Due Diligence

Taking reasonable steps to protect data.

Due Care

Implementing necessary security controls.

Attestation

Formal declaration confirming compliance.

Internal and External Audits

Verification of compliance by internal teams or third parties.

Automation

Automated compliance monitoring tools.

Privacy

Privacy laws regulate how personal data is handled.

Legal Implications

Organizations must comply with privacy laws at multiple levels:

Local or regional
National
Global

Data Subject

Individual whose data is being processed.

Controller vs Processor

Controller decides how data is used.

Processor handles data processing.

Data Ownership

Defines who legally owns data.

Data Inventory and Retention

Tracking stored data and defining how long it is kept.

Right to Be Forgotten

Legal right allowing individuals to request deletion of their personal data.

5.5 Audits and Assessments

Audits evaluate security controls and compliance.

Attestation

Formal confirmation that systems meet security requirements.

Internal Audits

Conducted by the organization.

Examples:

Compliance reviews
Audit committee reviews
Self-assessments

External Audits

Performed by independent organizations.

Examples:

Regulatory audits
Security assessments
Third-party audits

Penetration Testing

Simulated attacks used to evaluate security defenses.

Physical Penetration Testing

Testing physical security controls.

Offensive Testing

Actively attempting to compromise systems.

Defensive Testing

Evaluating incident response capabilities.

Integrated Testing

Combining multiple testing approaches.

Known Environment Testing

Tester has full system knowledge.

Partially Known Environment

Tester has limited knowledge.

Unknown Environment (Black Box Testing)

Tester has no prior knowledge of systems.

Reconnaissance

Information gathering before attacks.

Passive Reconnaissance

Gathering information without interacting with the target.

Active Reconnaissance

Direct interaction with the target system.

5.6 Security Awareness Practices

Security awareness programs educate employees to prevent security incidents.

Phishing Awareness

Training employees to recognize phishing attacks.

Phishing Campaigns

Simulated phishing attacks used for training.

Recognizing Phishing Attempts

Employees learn to identify suspicious emails.

Responding to Suspicious Messages

Employees report phishing attempts to security teams.

Anomalous Behavior Recognition

Recognizing unusual user behavior.

Types include:

Risky behavior
Unexpected behavior
Unintentional mistakes

User Guidance and Training

Security training topics include:

Security policies
Situational awareness
Insider threats
Password management
Removable media risks
Social engineering awareness
Operational security
Remote work security

Reporting and Monitoring

Security awareness programs include continuous monitoring.

Types include:

Initial training
Recurring training

Development

Designing security awareness programs.

Execution

Delivering training to employees.
